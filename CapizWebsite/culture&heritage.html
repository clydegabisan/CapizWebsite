CULTURE AND HERITAGE

Ang Panublion Museum

Hughes St., Roxas City, Capiz

The whitewashed museum was originally built as a water tank on 1910, through the efforts of President Pastor Alcasar, the third President of the town of Capiz (1908-1912), to provide water to the local residents during the dry season. It was constructed near the “municipio.” The water flowed from the roof of the two-storey municipal building to the water tank which was 11.5 meter in diameter and 6.10 meters in height, with walls that are 11 inches thick. It was luckily spared from the devastation of World War II because of its strong framework.

After the war, offices were built around the tank completely hiding it from view with only a water spout visible. In 1993, the offices were demolished and for the first time in three decades, the water tank was seen again.

"Panublion", a Hiligaynon term which means "heritage", is the official name of the museum. Over the years, it has outlived its purpose since the town came up with a modern water system. Thus, the birth of the Panublion Museum by Mayor Juliano Alba in the year 1992. It was named Panublion since it is considered as a gift from the past.

The Panublion Museum features the memorabilia of the prominent sons and daughters of the Province of Capiz and collections of religious and cultural icons, artifacts and artworks.

Birthplace of Manuel A. Roxas

Zamora St., Roxas City, Capiz

The two-story hardwood stone house of then Governor of Capiz and the First President of the Third Republic of the Philippines is located at Rizal St., corner Zamora St., in Roxas City. This house that have withstood the test of time was inherited by Roxas from his parents, Gerardo Roxas, Sr., and Rosario Acuña, which they also inherited from the parents of Gerardo Roxas. Today, the house is still under the care of the relatives of Roxas and it houses old furniture and memorabilia of the Roxas family.

Capiz Bridge

Roxas City, Capiz

The old and imposing Capiz Bridge has remained strong and firm since it was built in 1910. This bridge used to be a wooden carpenter bridge. Already more than a century old, it follows the classic European architectural design. Connecting the commercial center of the city to the main thoroughfare, traversing the Panay River, the bridge is a silent witness to various changes that ushered in progress and development in the city. Like the old bridge, equally alluring is the huge stretch of the Panay River that traverses the very heart of the city.

Rizal Monument

Roxas City, Capiz

This monument located in front of the Provincial Capitol is recognized as the second oldest monument of Rizal in the country. It was sculpted by Tomas Confesor of Manila and was built in 1911 through the efforts of then governor Jose Cortez Altavas. On its die are inscribed Spanish words: Patria, Justicia and Libertad which were names of Gov. Altavas' three daughters. The words also mean Homeland, Justice and Liberty respectively.

Capiz Provincial Capitol

Roxas City, Capiz

In the 3,000-square meter lot where the Capiz Provincial Capitol now stands used to be a two-story government-owned wooden house that was destroyed by a typhoon. Under the American regime, Gov. William Howard Taft appointed Simplicio Jugo Vidal as the first Civil Governor of Capiz after the Philippine Commission of the United States established the provincial government of Capiz on April 15, 1901 through the passage of Act 115 titled, “An Act Extending the Provisions of the Provincial Government Act to the Province of Capiz.” It was under Vidal’s 3-year term that the wooden house was rehabilitated.

It was in 1911, under Gov. Jose Cortez Altavas that the concrete building was constructed. In the same year, the Philippine flag was flown in front of the Capitol, and the monument of Jose Rizal was erected.

The third story of the Capitol was constructed between 1945-1949. The concrete Eagle design on the façade of the building which can be seen to this day, was set to symbolize the influence of the United States in Capiz.

Under Gov. Jose Borda in 1989, the Provincial Capitol was remodeled. The Governor’s Hall had major renovation and the windows were decorated with pios or Capiz shells that gave the building a grander look.

Immaculate Concepcion Metropolitan Cathedral

Roxas City, Capiz

Founded in 1707 by Augustinian missionaries, the Immaculate Conception Metropolitan Cathedral is one of the oldest churches in the Panay Island.

The church is standing in the heart of Roxas City facing the Panay River and the old Capiz Bridge; and is celebrated for standing the test of weather and time. Not only it has served as a place of worship for more than 300 years, but it has also become a destination for pilgrimage experience and faith-based tourism in the province.

This church was built with hardwood and bamboos before 1698. However, a strong typhoon on January 4, 1698 destroyed it and the convent. The stone church was then constructed from 1728 to 1732 but in the unfortunate year of 1787, it was destroyed by an earthquake.

From 1870 to 1885, it was rebuilt with stone and galvanized iron through the efforts of Fr. Apolinar Alvarez, O.S.A. And on the succeeding years, from 1885 to 1890, Fr. Lesmes Perez, O.S.A. made restorations by installing the ceiling with galvanized iron and plastering the walls with stucco and adding three grandiose altars.

This church was also considered as one of the most beautiful churches in the Philippines.

Filamer Christian University

Roxas City, Capiz

From being a home school, Filamer Christian University celebrates more than 100 years of Christian Education. It was founded in 1904 by Rev. Joseph Robbins and his wife, Cecilia Sainz, as a primary school for orphaned and homeless children. During the wage of the Second World War, Filamer closed and was used as garrison by the Japanese soldiers. When the civil government reconstituted after the war, the school reopened with a secondary level under the directory of the first Filipino principal, Jacoba Ibañez. In 1952, it was named Filamer Christian Institute, signifying the ties between the Filipinos and the Americans working together to run the institute. In 1985, it was officially named as Filamer Christian College. By July of 2010, it was granted the university status, thus named, Filamer Christian University.

Sta. Monica Church

Panay, Capiz

Built in 1774, this church in Panay town is about 250-foot long and 80-foot wide with walls made of coral blocks and are nine-foot thick. The central altar is an elaborate retablo of silver and hardwood in florid Baroque style. The lateral altars have intricately carved tiers of niches for images of saints. The five-story belfry houses a huge, old bell, surrounded by eight smaller bells. Popularly known as the Panay Bell, it is cast from 70 sacks of coins contributed by the townsfolk.  It is 7 ft. in diameter, has a height of 5 ft. and weighs 10.4 tons, and recognized as the Largest Catholic Church Bell in Southeast Asia, which sound can be heard from 8 kilometers away.

Sto. Tomas de Villanueva Church

Dao, Capiz

The church is made of coral stone, very refined and marble-like. It has one main nave and very spacious transept. The façade is characterized by the massive wall that contrasts with some rather slender columns. It is a one-story affair and has a very small Greek pediment which puts the whole façade off-balance. The main entrance, a semi-circular arch, is flanked on both sides by two almost equally large arched entrances separated by paired Tuscan columns mounted on heavily built rectangular pedestals. The cornice is slightly decorated with triglyphs.

St. Martin of Tours Church

Dumalag, Capiz

This century-old Church’s interior is shaped like a cross and is made from yellow sandstone. The Church’s six arched windows at each side with columns between them reaching the roof with buttresses at the side give it a different look.  It was finished in 1873 when Fr. Angel Abasolo was the parish priest of the town.  At the front left side of the church is the five-storey belfry housing five bells of different sizes, all made in 1881.

Other historical accounts also point to an earlier construction of the church. According to Henry Florida Funtecha, a historian from Barangay Duran, the first church and convent were constructed between 1600 and 1720. Under the leadership of Padre Agustin Duran in 1833, the church was constructed with yellow sandstone and its interior walls were decorated with Padre Juan Carlos’ paintings. This corroborated with Fr. Pedro G. Galende’s account in his book Angels in Stone: Augustinian Churches in the Philippines about the possibility that it was Padre Angel Abasolo who initiated the construction of the church, and having it rebuilt from a previous structure.

On January 10, 1875, the church was destroyed by a typhoon. In 1948, a strong earthquake heavily damaged the church and other structures in the town. Only the church’s walls and the belfry were left standing. A temporary church was built in front of the present structure until it was reconstructed in the 1950’s under Monsignor Jesus N. Frantilla as parish priest.

Balisong Hill

Natividad, Pilar, Capiz

Balisong Hill, also known as the Calupugan Hill, is a sloping hill in the barangay of San Esteban in Pilar Capiz surrounded by caves, fishponds and mangroves. It was named after the Balisong River which once flowed at the foot of the hill. 

The Balisong Hill served as a silent witness to a battle between the Spanish soldiers and the Filipino revolutionaries known in history as the Battle of Balisong. It was chosen by the revolutionary leaders as the site of their camp not only because it was a strategic location but that the surrounding provided a good source of food, water and big rocks that supplemented their meager ammunition. 

The Battle of Balisong began when Juan Arce, the revolutionary organizer in the area of Sara, Iloilo; Pilar, Cuartero, Dao and Pan-ay, was informed by his messenger of the arrival of the Spanish warship of 500 cazadores led by Capiz Gov. Juan Herrero. Upon Herrero’s order, the first group of cazadores moved forward and started the attack. When they were close enough, Juan Arce signaled to return fire. The Spaniards were better armed and had outnumbered the revolutionaries by 20 to 1. When they ran out of ammunition, they threw stones at their enemies causing minimal damage. Then they cut the anchoring vines of the 3-meter-long logs that they positioned on the summit of the hill, and rolled them to the cazadores. After three hours of intense fighting, Arce’s gun was hit by a bullet from the Spanish troops and bounced to his forehead. Before he died, he ordered Dalmacio Patricio, his right hand man to escape.

The revolutionaries lost Arce and Jorge Sinson, while the Spanish troops lost nearly 200 soldiers.
